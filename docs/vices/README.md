# The Vice Catalog

This is the index of recurring AI storytelling vices, the habits that AI models
fall into when they generate fiction, whether you are steering an assistant in a
chat window or building a narrative engine of your own. Each entry links to a
field-guide document with the tell (how to spot it), before/after examples in
varied genres, the root cause, and two kinds of fix: one for writers working in a
chat, one for developers wiring the fault out at the schema and architecture level.

Read the vices below, then see how the fixes fit together:

- [The Core Idea](../the-core-idea.md): why "don't do X" prohibitions fail, and
  how an affirmative pre-write commitment durably beats stubborn prose vices.
- [Cheatsheet](../cheatsheet.md) is the fast lookup for tells and fixes.

Vices are grouped into three families: sentence-level prose habits, dialogue and
voice habits, and larger structural and worldbuilding habits.

---

## Prose & sentence-level

Habits that live inside a sentence or a single beat: how the prose asserts,
hedges, glosses, and paces itself.

- [Contrastive Reveal (Not X, but Y)](contrastive-reveal.md). A clause negates a
  plausible alternative before asserting the "true" one, staging a twist the reader
  never actually expected.
- [Recap Chain](recap-chain.md). Beats open by re-listing prior state ("having
  done X, you now Y") instead of advancing on the new event.
- [Gesture Gloss](gesture-gloss.md). A physical action is shown, then a trailing
  clause interprets its meaning ("as if," "like someone who," "a sign of").
- [Aphorism & Oracle Voice](aphorism-and-oracle.md). Concrete beats get capped with
  portable maxims, and characters speak only in polished, pillow-stitchable proverbs.
- [Narrate-then-un-narrate](narrate-then-unnarrate.md). The narrator asserts a fact
  then retracts it in the same breath, selling false mystery as style.
- [Onomatopoeia Spam](onomatopoeia-spam.md). Comic-panel sound words land on nearly
  every beat, doing work the sentence should carry.
- [Forced Sensory Detail](forced-sensory-detail.md). Reflex chemistry-set smells
  (ozone, copper, iron) get stamped onto beats the point-of-view character could
  never name that way.
- [Register & Anachronism Leak](register-and-anachronism-leak.md). Wrong formality,
  pronoun, or modern slang leaks into an established voice or period, and the whole
  register drifts over a long session.

## Dialogue & voice

Habits in how characters speak: cadence, distinctiveness, and who is allowed to
talk at all.

- [Dialogue Fragmentation](dialogue-fragmentation.md). Short clauses fired in a row,
  each slammed shut by a full stop, so one connected idea reads as a chain of resets.
- [Uniform Laconic Voice](uniform-laconic-voice.md). Every character and moment comes
  out in the same clipped, withholding register, regardless of who is speaking.
- [Voice Homogeneity](voice-homogeneity.md). Strip the speaker tags and you can't
  tell who said what; the cast has no idiolect and collapses toward one flat tone.
- [Speaking for the Protagonist / Reader](speaking-for-the-protagonist.md). The model
  invents the point-of-view character's dialogue, choices, and feelings instead of
  rendering only the declared act.

## Structural & worldbuilding

Habits you only see in aggregate, across scenes, across a whole session, or across
the world itself.

- [Tonal Drift (Grimdark Creep)](tonal-drift.md). A warm or wondrous tone quietly
  cools into muted prestige-noir across a long session, one fine sentence at a time.
- [Self-Cannibalizing Repetition (Autophagy)](self-cannibalizing-repetition.md). Fed
  its own recent prose, the model imitates and amplifies its habits until imagery,
  vocabulary, and energy narrow and decay.
- [Everything Is a Crisis](everything-is-a-crisis.md). Every scene defaults to threat
  or conflict, erasing slice-of-life texture and stakes variety.
- [Inert World](inert-world.md). The setting never changes off-screen and has no
  ambient life; everything holds still and orbits the protagonist.
- [Epithet / Archetype Naming](epithet-naming.md). Names and titles spell out a
  character's theme ("the Brooding Swordsman," "Marked by Sorrow") instead of naming
  a person and letting action reveal them.
